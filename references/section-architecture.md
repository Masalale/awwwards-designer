# Section Architecture: Building the Argument

> **Invariant principle:** An Awwwards site is not a page with sections. It is an argument — a sequence of moments that builds toward a specific emotional and intellectual destination. Every section has a job. Generic sections (About, Services, Contact) are the enemy of this principle because they describe the brand instead of expressing it.
>
> **Current-era note:** The section types and sequences described here reflect what works at the current moment. New section paradigms will emerge. The principle — each section has a job derived from the brand metaphor, not from convention — is permanent.

---

## The Argument Model

Before naming a single section, answer this:

**What is the brand's central argument?**

Not: "We are a premium knife maker."
But: "Patience is a form of intelligence. The 40 hours that made this blade is the argument."

**What must the user feel at the end, that they did not feel at the beginning?**

Not: "They should know our services."
But: "They should feel the weight of careful work in their hands."

Every section is one step in moving the user from where they arrive to where that destination is.

---

## Section Types: Jobs, Not Names

Sections have jobs. The name follows the job — not the other way around.

### 1. The Threshold (Hero)
**Job:** Create the first impression that cannot be undone.
**What it must do in 3 seconds:** Communicate the brand's emotional trigger without explanation.
**What it must NOT do:** Explain what the brand does. That is for later. The threshold is felt, not read.
**Content:** As little as possible. Brand name. The signature interaction beginning. One visual element that could only belong to this brand.

### 2. The Premise
**Job:** State the brand's central argument without hedging.
**Tone:** Not marketing copy — a position. A declaration.
**Example (Kōen):** "40 hours. One blade. The mathematics of patience."
**Example (Stratum):** "We leave the formwork marks. The wall should show how it was made."
**What makes it Awwwards-level:** The copy is specific enough to be unprovable by a competitor. It cannot be copied and pasted onto a different brand.

### 3. The Demonstration
**Job:** Prove the premise through the signature interaction.
**This is where the twist lives at full expression.** If the invention is about scroll-driven forging stages, this section is the longest — the user spends the most time here.
**Length:** Should be the longest section on the page. The interaction earns time.
**Content:** The work, the process, the thing itself — experienced, not described.

### 4. The Evidence
**Job:** Shift from feeling to believing. Proof that the premise is true.
**Format:** Work samples, case studies, specifications, numbers — but framed through the brand lens, not as a portfolio grid.
**Example (Kōen):** Not "Portfolio" — a selection of blades, each shown at the forging stage where its character was decided.
**Example (Stratum):** Not "Projects" — each project shown as a cross-section: what the material is, where it came from, why it was chosen.

### 5. The Authority
**Job:** Remove doubt without announcing credentials.
**Tone:** Not "We have 20 years of experience." Instead: the thing you can only say if you've been doing this for 20 years.
**Example (Kōen):** A single line about the specific steel source, the forge temperature, the family lineage — the kind of detail that only the person who lived it would think to mention.

### 6. The Invitation
**Job:** Give the user something to do that extends the relationship.
**Not a CTA button.** An action that matches the brand's nature.
**Example (Kōen):** "Commission a blade. The process begins with a conversation about how you cook." (Not "Contact Us.")
**Example (Stratum):** "Tell us what material you want to leave visible. We start there." (Not "Get a Quote.")

## Deriving Sections from Brand Metaphor

The sections above are the skeleton. How you fill them depends on your three metaphors.

**From Metaphor 1 (Physical Sensation):** This determines the texture of transitions between sections. If the brand feels like cold steel, sections transition with precision and no overshoot. If the brand feels like living liquid, sections bleed into each other.

**From Metaphor 2 (Materiality):** This determines how content is presented. A brand made of geological strata presents evidence in layers. A brand made of frosted glass presents evidence partially obscured — revealed only on closer attention.

**From Metaphor 3 (Emotional Trigger):** This determines what the Invitation section asks for. The emotional destination of the site is the action you invite the user into.

## Anti-Patterns: Sections That Kill Awwwards Scores

**The Stats Strip:** "200+ projects / 15 years / 30 countries" — numbers without context, weight, or story. Remove entirely or replace with a single specific number that tells a deeper truth.

**The Generic About:** Three paragraphs about the team's "passion for craft" and "commitment to excellence." Replace with the premise section — a specific position the brand takes that excludes something.

**The Service Grid:** Four boxes with icons. "Strategy / Design / Development / Support." This is a menu, not an argument. Replace with the demonstration section.

**The Testimonial Carousel:** Three floating quotes from anonymous-feeling clients. Replace with one specific story of one specific project that embodies the brand's argument at its fullest.

**The Footer CTA:** "Ready to get started? Contact us today." Replace with the invitation — a specific action that makes sense for this brand's emotional trigger.

## Section Rhythm: Pacing the Argument

The sequence of section lengths creates rhythm. Rhythm creates the sense of building toward something.

**Short → Long → Short → Long → Short**

The signature interaction (Demonstration) is always the longest. Everything else breathes around it.

```
Threshold:    Short  (100vh — full screen, immediate)
Premise:      Short  (60–80vh — one powerful statement)
Demonstration: Long  (300–600vh — the interaction earns scroll time)
Evidence:     Medium (150–200vh — enough to convince, not overwhelm)
Authority:    Short  (40–60vh — quiet confidence)
Invitation:   Short  (80–100vh — clean, focused, no noise)
```

These are starting points. Derive the ratios from the brand's emotional trigger. A brand about patience can afford longer silences. A brand about energy needs shorter beats.

## The Section-to-Section Transition

How sections connect is as important as what they contain. Three transition types:

**Cut (hard transition):** Sections end cleanly. The next begins. Best for: brutalist, architectural, precision brands. Signals: nothing is hidden, everything is intentional.

**Bleed (continuous transition):** The signature interaction continues across section boundaries. The scroll experience is unbroken. Best for: narrative-driven, immersive, sensory brands. Signals: this is one continuous experience, not a series of stops.

**Threshold (moment transition):** Something specific happens at the section boundary — a visual event, a sound (if opt-in), a state change. Best for: brands with dramatic tension, brands where arrival in a new section deserves acknowledgment.

Your paradigm choice points to your transition type:
- **Scroll:** Bleed — the interaction is continuous
- **Layout:** Cut — each section is a new configuration
- **Loading/Reveal:** Threshold — each new section is an arrival
- **Hover:** Cut — sections are distinct territories
- **Typography/Cursor:** Bleed — the interaction is always present
